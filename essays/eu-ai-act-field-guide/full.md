# The EU AI Act, for the investment field

What it is, who it touches in investing, and what to do now — written for the people who run the capital, not the lawyers who advise them.

Reviewed June 2026 — incorporates the Digital Omnibus changes of 7 May 2026.

The EU AI Act doesn't ban your diligence stack — not Microsoft 365 Copilot in your inbox and your deck, not Claude or ChatGPT Enterprise on the analyst's second monitor, not Perplexity pulling sources for the IC memo, not Claude Code or OpenAI Codex spinning up an autonomous workflow over your data room. It asks you to know what each one is, what it sees, and who's accountable when it's wrong.

Foundation

## What the EU AI Act actually is

The **EU AI Act** (Regulation [(EU) 2024/1689](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj/eng)) is the world's first horizontal law on artificial intelligence. It was adopted on 13 June 2024 and entered into force on **1 August 2024**, with obligations phased in through [Article 113](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-113).

It is *risk-based*: the rules scale with what an AI system actually does, not with the technology behind it. In practice:

- **Minimal / limited risk** — Microsoft 365 Copilot summarising meeting notes; ChatGPT Enterprise or Claude drafting an IC memo at the firms that have moved past the default. This is where most investment-firm tools sit today.
- **High-risk** — AI that scores or ranks *people*: credit scoring of individual borrowers, life or health insurance pricing, CV screening for new hires, performance scoring of portfolio-company staff. This is where the compliance weight actually sits.
- **Company / stock / private-company analysis** — financial modelling, market sizing, competitive analysis, IC memo drafting, ESG screening, deal structuring, even reading every file in a data room with an agent. *Minimal risk.* The Act only tips into the higher tiers when an AI system starts scoring *identifiable individuals* (founders, employees, borrowers). Analysing the company is fine; ranking the people inside it is the line.
- **Prohibited** — social scoring by public authorities, untargeted face-scraping, emotion recognition at work or school, and other practices listed in [Article 5](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-5). Rare in finance, but worth knowing the line exists.

It also crosses borders: under [Article 2(1)(c)](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-2), what triggers the Act is where the *output* is used, not where the model, the data or the firm sits. Three concrete cases:

- A **London-based PE fund** uses a US-hosted large language model (LLM) to score EU founders for a Series B — *in scope*.
- A **Singapore family office** runs an LLM in Singapore, but the investment memo it produces is read by an EU-based principal — *in scope*.
- A **US VC's portfolio company** sells an AI hiring tool into Germany — *in scope*, and the GP's diligence should have caught it.

For any pan-European or globally-allocated firm, the Act effectively follows the portfolio.

### What the Act does *not* do

**It does not ban LLMs, copilots, or AI agents.** Most investment work sits in minimal or limited risk.

**It does not require pre-approval of internal AI tools by any regulator.** No filings, no licence, no sandbox entry for an IC drafting tool.

**It does not override NDAs, Material Non-Public Information (MNPI) rules, or fiduciary duty.** Those already govern what you can paste into a model.

**It is not GDPR repeated.** [GDPR](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2016/679/oj) governs personal data; the AI Act governs the AI system itself. Both apply in parallel.

**[2 Feb 2025](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-4) — AI literacy as a duty.** Anyone in the firm who touches an AI tool — analyst pasting an LP list into ChatGPT, associate using Claude to draft a memo, partner running Copilot in Outlook — needs documented basic training. A 60-minute session with sign-off is enough for most firms.

**[2 Aug 2025](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj/eng) — GPAI (general-purpose AI) rules + governance live.** The model providers (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Mistral, Meta) carry the bulk. For deploying firms it shows up indirectly: new contract clauses, training-data summaries you can request, copyright reps you should look for when signing.

**2 Dec 2026 — AI-content marking + new Art. 5 ban.** Provider-side marking of AI-generated content under [Article 50(2)](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-50) shifts from August to December 2026 under the Omnibus, and a new [Article 5](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-5) prohibition on AI-generated non-consensual intimate imagery (NCII) and child sexual abuse material (CSAM) is added on the same date. Deployer transparency duties under Art. 50 (the "label it as AI" obligations on you) still apply from 2 Aug 2026 — only the provider-side marking under 50(2) moved.

**2 Dec 2027 — high-risk core rules (deferred from 2 Aug 2026).** The date that matters if anything you or your portfolio builds scores or ranks people (the [Annex III](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/annex-3) list).

**2 Aug 2028 — embedded high-risk products (deferred from 2 Aug 2027).** AI baked into regulated hardware or safety-critical products ([Article 6(1)](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-6), Annex I).

**A note on timing (June 2026).** The [Digital Omnibus on AI](https://www.consilium.europa.eu/en/press/press-releases/2026/05/07/artificial-intelligence-council-and-parliament-agree-to-simplify-and-streamline-rules/) — amendments agreed in principle on 7 May 2026 and endorsed by the European Parliament on 16 June 2026 — pushes the high-risk deadlines back. Annex III high-risk obligations move from 2 Aug 2026 to 2 Dec 2027; embedded high-risk products from 2 Aug 2027 to 2 Aug 2028. The literacy and prohibited-use duties (Feb 2025) and the GPAI rules (Aug 2025) are unchanged. The deferral only takes legal effect once published in the Official Journal (expected mid-2026), so until then 2 Aug 2026 technically remains the live date. Start now regardless — the binding work is the inventory, and it does not get easier with time.

Risk tiers

## What each tier looks like in practice

A compact reference — definitions are in Section 1.

**Prohibited.** Scoring a target's employees by inferred emotion from recorded Zoom calls to "value the team." Don't.

**High-risk.** An insurance portfolio company pricing life policies with AI; a credit-fund underwriting tool that ranks individual borrowers; a portfolio company's AI-driven CV screener for hiring. Triggers the full stack: risk management, data governance, technical documentation, human oversight, post-market monitoring, conformity assessment.

**Limited risk.** A chatbot on the LP portal answering FAQs; AI-generated imagery in a fund's annual letter. Duty: label it as AI.

**Minimal risk.** ChatGPT, Claude or Gemini for memo drafting; Copilot for Excel modelling; transcription on IC calls; Claude Code or OpenAI Codex used internally to automate diligence-file processing — plus, explicitly, *end-to-end company and stock analysis*.

**GPAI (general-purpose AI models).** A separate track for the foundation models themselves — GPT-4 series, GPT-5, Claude, Gemini, Mistral, Llama. Transparency, training-data summaries, copyright compliance, and (for the largest systemic models) safety evaluations. Mostly a *provider* duty; firms inherit it through the contracts they sign.

Why it matters

## Why it matters to investing

Three angles a GP, CIO or family-office principal actually cares about.

### a) Using AI inside the firm — assistants vs agents

There are two kinds of AI tools. *Assistants* — like ChatGPT or Microsoft Copilot — help a person do their job, but the person makes every decision. *Agents* — like Claude Code or custom workflows — can work on their own across many systems: they can open files, read data, write memos, and send messages without a person watching every step.

The AI Act treats both the same way, but agents carry more risk. They touch more systems, see more private data, and need more places where a person must check the work before it goes further. A simple screening tool can accidentally start ranking people, and that pushes it into the high-risk category.

### b) Investing in AI companies

When you buy a company that uses AI, checking the AI Act is now a standard part of due diligence. For any target whose product touches EU users, five questions to answer:

- What risk category is the AI product in?
- Does it have proper approvals if it is high-risk?
- Which AI model runs underneath it?
- Where did the training data come from?
- How many EU customers does it have?

Finding an AI Act problem *after* you have bought the company is the new version of finding a data-privacy (GDPR) problem after a deal closes. It is a hidden cost that can wipe out the value of the investment.

### c) Data, confidentiality, vendor stack

The AI Act works alongside existing data rules ([*GDPR*](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2016/679/oj)) and, for financial firms, [*DORA*](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2022/2554/oj). The biggest danger for most firms is not the AI Act itself — it is letting confidential deal data leak out through a hosted AI tool. Pasting a private document into a personal ChatGPT account is very different from using the same tool on a company contract with data kept in the EU and no data stored by the provider. Same task, very different danger.

For VC, PE and private credit general partners

### GPs — VC, PE & Private Credit

As a GP you are almost always a *deployer*, not a provider. Sourcing, screening, IC drafting and monitoring sit in **minimal or limited risk** under the Act. The real exposure is two steps removed from your laptop — and it splits cleanly along strategy.

**VC and PE — risk travels with the deal.** Equity-backed companies that build AI which **scores or ranks people** (CV screening, employee assessment, fraud/credit decisions) sit in the high-risk tier. The obligations don't disappear at close — they follow the cap table.[Annex III](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/annex-3) is the reference. It does not matter where your fund is domiciled: any portfolio company selling into the EU pulls you into scope.

**Private credit — you may be the high-risk deployer.** AI underwriting models that score individuals or SMEs are a textbook [Annex III](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/annex-3) high-risk case. If a borrower runs that model, it's their obligation. If the **fund itself runs it** — direct lending, NAV, ABL with an in-house scoring layer — the fund is operating as a high-risk deployer in its own right: different governance stack, conformity assessment, logging, human oversight.

**Internal agents — same tier, wider surface.** Claude Code, OpenAI Codex, n8n / LangGraph workflows reading the data room end-to-end and drafting memos don't change your tier, but they widen the confidentiality surface and multiply the places a human-in-the-loop checkpoint needs to sit.

It does not matter where your fund is domiciled: any portfolio company or borrower selling into the EU, or any LP receiving your output in the EU, pulls you into scope.

Where you can get burned

- **Portfolio-company DD that doesn't ask the AI Act question.** A clean SaaS company can ship a CV-screener as a feature update post-close and flip into the high-risk tier overnight. You won't have priced this.
- **Agents drifting into ranking people.** A screening workflow that started as "summarise this founder's deck" becomes "score these 200 founders on coachability." Once it ranks identifiable individuals, the tier changes.
- **Confidential portfolio data in personal LLM accounts.** Confidential Information Memorandum (CIM) pasted into a personal ChatGPT account vs the same task on an enterprise tenancy with zero-retention is the same workflow, very different exposure — and very different posture in front of an LP.
- **No AI-literacy paper trail.** The Feb 2025 duty is the easiest box to tick and the easiest one for an LP to ask about. Don't be caught without sign-off sheets.
- **Private-credit underwriting models built in-house.** If the fund itself runs an AI scoring model on borrowers — including SME borrowers — that's the fund operating as a high-risk deployer, not minimal-risk. The model is still commercially viable — the change is that the fund must accept the full high-risk deployer obligations (conformity assessment, logging, human oversight) rather than treating it as minimal-risk.

Monday morning

- —**Inventory every AI tool** the team touches — browser ChatGPT, M365 Copilot, Claude, Cursor, Codex, autonomous agents. You can't classify what you can't see.
- —**Run AI-literacy training** (60 minutes, sign-off, refreshed annually). In force since Feb 2025.
- —**Add an AI Act block to your DD questionnaire** for every new investment or new borrower. Which tier, conformity assessment if high-risk, GPAI provider underneath, training-data provenance, EU deployment footprint.
- —**Govern your agents explicitly.** For each agentic workflow: scope, data access, human checkpoints, kill switch. One page per agent.
- —**Vendor contracts:** zero-retention, EU data residency where possible, sub-processors disclosed, right to audit.
- —**Name a single owner** — usually the COO or General Counsel — for the inventory, the policy, and the regulator relationship.

For LPs across fund commitments, co-investments and secondaries

### LPs — capital allocators

As an LP you are rarely a deployer in the legal sense — you don't run the AI tool that triggers an obligation. But your **indirect exposure** is real, and it shifts with the mode you're operating in.

**Fund commitments.** Exposure runs through two layers: the GPs you back, and the portfolio companies they hold. A GP that misclassifies the risk tier of a portfolio company's AI product is a GP that's mispricing it. An AI Act surprise after close — a product told to suspend, a fine, a forced redesign — is the new GDPR surprise, and you'll feel it in the mark.

**Co-investments.** You become a direct shareholder alongside the GP, often without the GP's full DD wrap and on a faster timeline. AI Act risk on the asset sits partly with you; your own IC memo and the Share Purchase Agreement (SPA) reps need to carry it. Don't outsource the AI question to the lead — the lead's classification is the lead's view, not a substitute for your own.

**Secondaries — LP-led and GP-led continuation vehicles.** You're buying after the AI product was built and classified by someone else. You inherit any misclassification without ever having sat on the IC. The diligence muscle is different: read the inventory, read the regulator correspondence file, and ask what has changed since the last GP reclassification.

**Fund-of-funds and gatekeepers** add one more layer — your AI-literacy and ODD asks need to cascade to the underlying GPs, not just your direct counterparties.

**Sophisticated LPs running single-asset direct deals** are operating as a GP for that deal; read the [GP lane](/ai-act/gp).

You also need to know whether the GP is using AI on *your* data: LP-portal chatbots, AI-drafted quarterly letters, AI-generated imagery in fund decks. Limited risk, but a transparency duty applies — the GP must label them as AI.

Where you can get burned

- **GPs without an AI inventory.** If the manager can't tell you which AI tools the team uses, they can't classify their own exposure — and neither can you.
- **Portfolio companies that flip into high-risk silently.** A product update adds CV screening or credit scoring; the AI Act treatment changes; the GP doesn't escalate. You hear about it in a write-down.
- **Co-investing on the lead's DD without asking the AI Act question.** The SPA reps carry equally to you. Faster timelines are not an excuse for inheriting the lead's classification untested.
- **Secondary purchases inheriting a stale AI classification.** What was minimal-risk at fund formation may be high-risk by the time you buy in — and the prior classification doesn't bind the regulator.
- **Reps and warranties that don't cover AI.** Standard SPA reps were written before the Act. Without an AI Act rep, recovery against the seller post-close is harder.

Monday morning

- —**Add an AI section to ODD.** Five questions: (1) full inventory of AI tools, (2) named owner for AI compliance, (3) AI-literacy training records since Feb 2025, (4) policy for agents, (5) approach to high-risk portfolio-company products. For co-invests and secondaries, run the AI Act question yourself — don't rely on the lead's or the seller's classification.
- —**Add an AI Act side letter clause** — annual disclosure of any portfolio-company AI product classified as high-risk, any regulator contact, any incident.
- —**Push GPs on vendor contracts.** Zero-retention, EU data residency, and a right to audit for the LP's data specifically (LP lists, capital-account info).
- —**Flag AI-labelled LP comms.** If a quarterly letter or fund deck contains AI-generated text or imagery, the GP owes you a label. Note it in your file.
- —**For your own team:** AI literacy if you use AI in screening or monitoring GPs (most modern LPs do).

For family-office principals and COOs

### Family offices

Family offices are **not a separately regulated category** under the Act. You inherit deployer duties whenever you use an AI tool — which today is constantly. Most FO work — memo drafting, market screening, deal-analysis copilots, document review with an assistant — sits firmly in **minimal or limited risk**.

**A note on cross-border structures.** Most family offices operate across jurisdictions. The Act follows the output, not the office — a Singapore-based principal reading an AI-generated memo in Geneva is in scope.

**Where to start, practically.** For most single-family offices, [Claude for Work](https://www.anthropic.com/enterprise) is the simplest starting point — one contract, EU data residency available, zero-retention on request, no IT infrastructure required. [ChatGPT Enterprise](https://openai.com/enterprise) and [Microsoft 365 Copilot](https://www.microsoft.com/microsoft-365/copilot) are equally defensible if the office already lives inside Google Workspace or Microsoft 365 — the choice is procurement, not safety.

**The AI you didn't buy.** Third-party platform AI features — the AI your private bank (UBS, Julius Baer, Pictet, Lombard Odier), portfolio-management platform (Addepar, Allvue) or CRM (Salesforce, DealCloud) has quietly enabled — fall under the same rules. You are the *deployer*. Three questions to send the vendor: what tier is this running on, does it train on my prompts, and where does my data go? Their answer belongs in your vendor register (see [Article 05 — FO lane](/ai-act/articles/defensibility/family-office)).

Where it gets more interesting is **agents and AI investing workflows**. An agent that screens public-market tickers overnight, ranks private deals from your CRM, drafts outreach, or pulls a watchlist together is still fine under the Act — but it now needs an inventory entry, a human-in-the-loop checkpoint and an audit trail. Not because Brussels says so for FOs specifically, but because that is the only way you can answer "who decided this?" six months later.

The duty that applies to every FO regardless of size is **AI literacy** (in force since Feb 2025): everyone who touches an AI tool needs documented basic training. The rest of the Act mostly passes over you *unless* you start **scoring identifiable individuals** — founder background checks, key-person scoring, a "should we hire this person for the operating team" AI screener. That crosses into [GDPR](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2016/679/oj) profiling and, depending on the use case, AI Act high-risk Annex III territory. Don't.

*If you also use AI for your personal portfolio separately from the FO, the [HNW lane](/ai-act/hnwi) covers where the personal-use carve-out begins and ends.*

Where you can get burned

- **"We're too small for this."** AI-literacy is size-blind. A two-person FO running ChatGPT on portfolio analysis is in scope for the literacy duty.
- **"Our agent does the screening overnight."** Autonomous agents are fine, but they need an inventory entry, a human-in-the-loop checkpoint and an audit log of what they touched and why. Without it, you can't answer your auditor — or, where you have external co-investors, your partners.
- **AI-scored founder or key-person checks.** The moment the workflow scores a named individual (founder, GP, candidate ops hire, beneficiary), you're in profiling territory — [GDPR Art. 22](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2016/679/oj) and AI Act Annex III bite even for a single-family office.
- **Confidential family data in personal AI accounts.** Net worth, trust structures, beneficial ownership — in a personal ChatGPT account these become training data unless you're on a zero-retention enterprise tier.
- **Direct deals into AI companies without AI Act DD.** A direct minority in an AI startup needs the same screening [a GP would do](/ai-act/gp) — risk tier, conformity, GPAI vendor, EU footprint.

Monday morning

- —**One-page AI policy** — what tools are approved, what data may never go in, who to ask.
- —**Named owner** — usually the COO or the principal's chief of staff.
- —**AI-literacy session for everyone including the principal** — 60 minutes, sign-off, and inventory of all tools in use including personal devices.
- —**Agent register** — separate from the tool inventory: which agents run unattended, what data they touch, who reviews the output, how often.
- —**Scoring identifiable individuals by AI** requires a documented human-in-the-loop and a lawful basis under GDPR — and moves you out of the minimal-risk tier. Applies to founders, candidates and beneficiaries equally.
- —**Upgrade to enterprise AI tenancies** with zero-retention and EU residency if you're handling family personal data.

For CIOs and portfolio managers

### CIOs & PMs

Most investment work on a CIO's desk — summarising 10-Ks, building DCFs, screening tickers, drafting IC memos, ESG screening — is **minimal risk** under the AI Act itself.

The binding constraints sit in adjacent regimes the AI Act overlays:

- **[MAR (Reg 596/2014)](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2014/596/oj).** Pasting a not-yet-public earnings call, an analyst's draft note, or a leaked deal term into a third-party LLM can amount to unlawful disclosure of inside information. The vendor's training and logging policy matters.
- **[MiFID II RTS 6 (Reg 2017/589)](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg_del/2017/589/oj).** Already in force. Any AI that places, modifies or cancels orders without a human in the loop triggers the existing algo regime — pre-trade controls, kill switches, annual self-assessment.
- **[SR 11-7](https://www.federalreserve.gov/supervisionreg/srletters/sr1107.htm) (US) and [SS1/23](https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/prudential-regulation/publication/2023/may/model-risk-management-principles-for-banks-ss) (UK).** Model-risk expectations already apply to anything used in investment decisions. The AI Act adds documentation, not new substance.

[ESMA's 30 May 2024 public statement](https://www.esma.europa.eu/document/public-statement-ai-and-investment-services) is explicit: existing MiFID conduct rules already cover AI in investment services. The AI Act layers **transparency** and **AI-literacy** on top.

Where you can get burned

- **Material Non-Public Information (MNPI) into third-party LLMs.** The single highest-consequence mistake on a public-equity desk. Treat it as an MAR incident waiting to happen, regardless of the AI Act.
- **AI in the order chain without RTS 6 coverage.** A "smart" execution helper that adjusts orders crosses into algo-trading territory and inherits the full algo governance stack.
- **Model inventory that excludes LLMs.** Your existing model-risk inventory was built for quant models. LLMs and AI agents need to be in it — same template, same controls.
- **Suitability and best-ex policies silent on AI.** If AI touches the advice chain, the policy needs to say so. ESMA expects it.

Monday morning

- —**Extend the model-risk inventory** to cover every LLM and agent in use — same template, same controls.
- —**Hard rule on MNPI** — no inside information into third-party LLMs. Write it, train on it, audit it.
- —**AI clause in suitability and best-execution policies.** Name what AI may touch and where human sign-off is mandatory.
- —**AI-literacy for the investment team.** Tailored — not the generic deck for ops.
- —**Vendor due diligence** on the LLM provider: zero-retention, EU residency, no training on your prompts.
- —**Log every AI tool used in the advice chain.** Auditable, ESMA-ready.

For individuals managing their own money

### HNW individuals

Running ChatGPT, Claude or a local model on your own portfolio at home is personal use under [Article 2(10)](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-2) — outside the AI Act. The carve-out is about who the output is for: keep it for yourself and your household, you're outside. The moment it informs someone else's investment decision in the EU, you're in.

The carve-out is *narrow*. It ends the moment you have employees, run a family office, manage capital for anyone else, or your output is used in the EU by someone who does.

**What about publishing — Substack, LinkedIn, a personal blog?** Sharing your own research, opinions or AI-investing workflow notes publicly is commentary, not a personalised investment service. It does not put you inside the AI Act *as a deployer for your readers*, and it does not turn you into a regulated adviser as long as you're not giving personalised recommendations to identified people for a fee. Three things can still tip it: (i) you start advising named readers on their specific portfolio — that's potentially regulated investment advice under MiFID, separate from the AI Act; (ii) you publish AI-generated images, audio or video — from 2 Aug 2026 you owe a transparency label under [Article 50](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-50), whatever your status; (iii) you process personal data about people you write about (founders, executives) — [GDPR](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2016/679/oj) applies regardless.

And **[GDPR](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2016/679/oj) still applies** — independent of the AI Act — whenever you process personal data about anyone other than yourself: founders you research, employees of companies you analyse, co-investors you compare notes with.

Where you can get burned

- **"Just helping a friend."** The moment you give a friend a personal investment recommendation built on AI analysis, you're no longer in the personal-use carve-out — and potentially crossing into regulated investment-advice territory under MiFID, separately from the AI Act.
- **Hiring your first analyst.** A single part-time hire tips you into deployer status. AI literacy applies, and you need a written policy on what data the analyst may put into which tools.
- **Personal AI accounts holding net-worth data.** Outside the AI Act, but a major GDPR and confidentiality risk if the consumer tier trains on your prompts. Pay for the enterprise tier.

Monday morning

- —**Know the carve-out line.** One sentence in your own notes — "I am personal-use under Article 2(10) until I hire, share, or advise."
- —**Pay for enterprise AI tiers** with zero-retention if you process anything sensitive — financial accounts, founder data, family information.
- —**Keep AI out of MNPI handling.** If you ever access non-public issuer information through a board seat or a relationship, no third-party LLM.
- —**If you're considering hiring** even one analyst, draft the one-page AI policy first. It's easier than retrofitting.

For independent advisors and ad-hoc wealth services

### Independent advisors

As an independent advisor — fractional CIO, boutique IR, independent investment consultant, ad-hoc analyst — you're a **deployer** the moment an output reaches a client. The personal-use carve-out doesn't apply to you.

Most of what you do (memo drafting, market analysis, deal screening, IC support) is **minimal or limited risk**. The duties that bite hardest for a small advisory practice are **AI literacy** (Feb 2025, every person in the practice) and **vendor governance** (zero-retention, EU residency for client data).

Confidentiality is the existential risk here. A single client memo pasted into a consumer LLM that retains and trains on prompts can end a boutique. The AI Act elevates this from "bad practice" to "documented obligation."

Where you can get burned

- **Consumer LLM accounts handling client data.** Free or personal-tier ChatGPT and Claude with retention on. Catastrophic for a confidentiality-led practice.
- **No written AI policy in your engagement letters.** If you use AI to draft client deliverables, your engagement letter should say so. Transparency duty plus client expectation management.
- **Sub-contractors using their own tools.** If you bring in a freelance analyst, their personal AI stack becomes your problem.
- **Crossing into regulated investment advice without realising.** AI output dressed as a "recommendation" for a specific client can attract MiFID licensing requirements separately from the AI Act.

Monday morning

- —**Upgrade to enterprise tiers** for every AI tool — zero-retention, EU residency, no training on prompts. Non-negotiable.
- —**AI-literacy session** for every person in the practice, including freelancers and contractors. Sign-off sheet on file.
- —**One-page AI policy** covering approved tools, data handling, MNPI, and what gets disclosed to clients.
- —**Add an AI clause to engagement letters** — what AI you use, for what, and how client data is handled.
- —**If any deliverable could be read as personalised investment advice**, check with counsel on MiFID licensing before scaling the service line.

Wealth managers, fund administrators, placement agents and IC or board members aren't separate lanes here — they inherit from the closest fit (Advisor, CIO, or the relevant GP/LP mode).

Action

## What to do now

- —**Inventory your AI.** Browser-tab ChatGPT, Copilot in M365, Claude / Cursor / Codex on engineering laptops, and any autonomous agents. You can't classify what you can't see.
- —**Run AI literacy training.** In force since Feb 2025. Sixty minutes, sign-off, refreshed annually.
- —**Review vendor contracts.** Zero-retention, EU data residency where possible, sub-processors, GPAI-provider terms, right to audit.
- —**Update IC and risk policies.** Name what AI may and may not touch in screening, diligence, IC memos and monitoring — and where human sign-off is mandatory.
- —**Govern your agents specifically.** For any Claude Code, Codex, n8n or LangGraph workflow, document scope, data access, human checkpoints and a kill switch.
- —**Map high-risk use cases early.** Hiring, credit, insurance, employee assessment — full high-risk treatment by Dec 2027 (deferred from Aug 2026 under the Digital Omnibus).
- —**Name an internal owner.** One person — usually the COO or General Counsel — who holds the inventory, the policy, and the relationship with regulators.

Enforcement

## Penalties and who enforces

**Fines** ([Article 99](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-99)), applied to worldwide annual turnover or a fixed cap — whichever is higher:

- **Prohibited practices (Art. 5 breach)** — up to **€35M or 7%** of total worldwide annual turnover (Art. 99(3)).
- **Breach of other obligations** (high-risk duties, transparency under Art. 50, deployer duties under Art. 26) — up to **€15M or 3%** (Art. 99(4)).
- **Supplying incorrect or misleading information** to notified bodies or national competent authorities — up to **€7.5M or 1%** (Art. 99(5)).
- **SMEs and start-ups** — under Art. 99(6), the fine is capped at the *lower* of the euro figure or the percentage, not the higher.

**Who enforces, in practice:**

- The [**EU AI Office**](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/ai-office) (inside the European Commission) — sole enforcer for general-purpose AI model providers: OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Mistral, Meta and the rest.
- **National competent authorities** in each Member State — enforce against deployers (the investment firm using the AI). Each country is naming its own; some are existing data-protection authorities, some are new bodies. Designation is still in progress as of June 2026.
- For regulated finance, the existing sectoral regulators — **ESMA, EBA, EIOPA** at EU level and national equivalents (BaFin, AMF, FCA-equivalents) — are expected to lead supervision of AI used in regulated activities, coordinating with the AI Office. ESMA has already published its first guidance (see Sources).

*One-line takeaway:* enforcement against an investment firm will almost certainly come through your existing financial regulator, not a separate AI regulator.
