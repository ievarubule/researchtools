# The EU AI Act, for the investment field

What it is, why it matters in investing, and what to do now — written for the people who run the capital, not the lawyers who advise them. Read the article straight through, then jump to your lane at the end (GP, LP, family office, CIO, HNW, independent advisor).

Reviewed June 2026 — incorporates the Digital Omnibus changes of 7 May 2026.

The EU AI Act doesn't ban your diligence stack — not Microsoft 365 Copilot in your inbox and your deck, not Claude or ChatGPT Enterprise on the analyst's second monitor, not Perplexity pulling sources for the IC memo, not Claude Code or OpenAI Codex spinning up an autonomous workflow over your data room. It asks you to know what each one is, what it sees, and who's accountable when it's wrong.

Foundation

## What the EU AI Act actually is

The **EU AI Act** (Regulation [(EU) 2024/1689](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj/eng)) is the world's first horizontal law on artificial intelligence. It was adopted on 13 June 2024 and entered into force on **1 August 2024**, with obligations phased in through [Article 113](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-113).

It is *risk-based*: the rules scale with what an AI system actually does, not the technology behind it.

- **Minimal / limited risk** — Microsoft 365 Copilot summarising meeting notes; ChatGPT Enterprise or Claude drafting an IC memo at the firms that have moved past the default. This is where most investment-firm tools sit today.
- **High-risk** — AI that scores or ranks *people*: credit scoring of individual borrowers, CV screening, performance scoring of portfolio-company staff. The list lives in [Annex III](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/annex-3).
- **Company / stock / private-company analysis** — financial modelling, market sizing, IC memo drafting, ESG screening, even reading every file in a data room with an agent. *Minimal risk in practical terms* for investment workflows that analyse companies rather than scoring identifiable individuals. The Act only tips into the higher tiers when an AI system starts scoring *identifiable individuals* — founders, employees or borrowers.
- **Prohibited** — social scoring by public authorities, untargeted face-scraping, emotion recognition at work or school, and other practices listed in [Article 5](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-5).

It also crosses borders: under [Article 2(1)(c)](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-2), what triggers the Act is where the *output* is used, not where the model, the data or the firm sits. A Singapore family office whose memo is read by an EU-based principal is in scope. A London PE fund scoring EU founders with a US-hosted large language model (LLM) is in scope.

What it does not do

## What the Act does not do

**It does not ban LLMs, copilots, or AI agents.** Most investment work sits in minimal or limited risk.

**It does not require pre-approval of internal AI tools by any regulator.** No filings, no licence, no sandbox entry for an IC drafting tool.

**It does not override NDAs, Material Non-Public Information (MNPI) rules, or fiduciary duty.** Those already govern what you can paste into a model.

**It is not GDPR repeated.** [GDPR](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2016/679/oj) governs personal data; the AI Act governs the AI system itself. Both apply in parallel.

Risk tiers

## What each tier looks like in practice

**Prohibited.** Scoring a target's employees by inferred emotion from recorded Zoom calls to "value the team." Don't.

**High-risk.** An insurance portfolio company pricing life policies with AI; a credit-fund underwriting tool that ranks individual borrowers; a portfolio company's AI-driven CV screener. Triggers the full stack: risk management, data governance, technical documentation, human oversight, post-market monitoring.

**Limited risk.** A chatbot on the LP portal; AI-generated imagery in a fund's annual letter. Duty: label it as AI ([Art. 50](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-50)).

**Minimal risk.** ChatGPT, Claude or Gemini for memo drafting; Copilot for Excel modelling; transcription on IC calls; Claude Code or OpenAI Codex automating diligence-file processing — plus end-to-end company and stock analysis.

**GPAI (general-purpose AI).** A separate track for the foundation models themselves — GPT-4 series, GPT-5, Claude, Gemini, Mistral, Llama. Mostly a *provider* duty; firms inherit it through the contracts they sign.

Why it matters

## Why it matters to investing

### Using AI inside the firm — assistants vs agents

Two kinds of tools. *Assistants* — ChatGPT, Claude, Microsoft Copilot — help a person do the job; the person makes every call. *Agents* — Claude Code, Codex, custom workflows — run across systems on their own: open files, read data, write memos, send messages, no one watching every step.

The Act treats both the same way, but agents carry more risk. They touch more systems, see more data, and need more places where a human checks the work before it moves on. A simple screening tool can drift into ranking people — and that pushes it into high-risk territory.

### Investing in AI companies

When you buy a company that uses AI, checking the AI Act is now a standard part of due diligence. You need to know: what risk category is the AI product in? Does it have proper approvals if it is high-risk? Which AI model runs underneath it? Where did the training data come from? And how many EU customers does it have?

Finding an AI Act problem *after* you have bought the company is the new version of finding a data-privacy (GDPR) problem after a deal closes. It is a hidden cost that can wipe out the value of the investment.

### Data, confidentiality, vendor stack

The AI Act works alongside existing data rules ([*GDPR*](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2016/679/oj)) and, for financial firms, [*DORA*](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2022/2554/oj). The biggest danger for most firms is not the AI Act itself — it is letting confidential deal data leak out through a hosted AI tool. Pasting a private document into a personal ChatGPT account is very different from using the same tool on a company contract with data kept in the EU and no data stored by the provider. Same task, very different danger.

**[2 Feb 2025](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-4) — AI literacy as a duty.** Anyone in the firm who touches an AI tool needs documented basic training. A 60-minute session with sign-off is enough for most firms.

**[2 Aug 2025](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj/eng) — GPAI rules + governance live.** The model providers (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Mistral, Meta) carry the bulk. For deploying firms it shows up indirectly: contract clauses, training-data summaries, copyright reps.

**2 Dec 2026 — AI-content marking + new Art. 5 ban.** Provider-side marking of AI-generated content under [Article 50(2)](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-50) shifts to December 2026 under the Omnibus, and a new [Article 5](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-5) prohibition on AI-generated non-consensual intimate imagery (NCII) and child sexual abuse material (CSAM) is added on the same date. Deployer transparency duties under Art. 50 still apply from 2 Aug 2026 — only provider-side marking moved.

**2 Dec 2027 — high-risk core rules (deferred from 2 Aug 2026).** The date that matters if anything you or your portfolio builds scores or ranks people (the [Annex III](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/annex-3) list).

**2 Aug 2028 — embedded high-risk products (deferred from 2 Aug 2027).** AI baked into regulated hardware or safety-critical products ([Article 6(1)](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-6), Annex I).

**A note on timing (June 2026).** The [Digital Omnibus on AI](https://www.consilium.europa.eu/en/press/press-releases/2026/05/07/artificial-intelligence-council-and-parliament-agree-to-simplify-and-streamline-rules/) — agreed in principle on 7 May 2026 and endorsed by the European Parliament on 16 June 2026 — pushes the high-risk deadlines back. Annex III high-risk obligations move from 2 Aug 2026 to 2 Dec 2027; embedded high-risk products from 2 Aug 2027 to 2 Aug 2028. The literacy and prohibited-use duties (Feb 2025) and the GPAI rules (Aug 2025) are unchanged. The deferral only takes legal effect once published in the Official Journal (expected mid-2026), so until then 2 Aug 2026 technically remains the live date. Start now regardless — the binding work is the inventory, and it does not get easier with time.

Action

## What to do now

- —**Inventory your AI.** Browser-tab ChatGPT, Copilot in M365, Claude / Cursor / Codex on engineering laptops, and any autonomous agents. You can't classify what you can't see.
- —**Run AI literacy training.** In force since Feb 2025. Sixty minutes, sign-off, refreshed annually.
- —**Review vendor contracts.** Zero-retention, EU data residency where possible, sub-processors, GPAI-provider terms, right to audit.
- —**Update IC and risk policies.** Name what AI may and may not touch in screening, diligence, IC memos and monitoring — and where human sign-off is mandatory.
- —**Govern your agents specifically.** For any Claude Code, Codex, n8n or LangGraph workflow, document scope, data access, human checkpoints and a kill switch.
- —**Map high-risk use cases early.** Hiring, credit, insurance, employee assessment — full high-risk treatment by Dec 2027 (deferred from Aug 2026 under the Digital Omnibus).
- —**Name an internal owner.** One person — usually the COO or General Counsel — who holds the inventory, the policy, and the relationship with regulators.

Enforcement

## Penalties and who enforces

**Fines** ([Article 99](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-99)), applied to worldwide annual turnover or a fixed cap — whichever is higher:

- **Prohibited practices (Art. 5 breach)** — up to **€35M or 7%** (Art. 99(3)).
- **Other obligations** (high-risk, transparency, deployer duties) — up to **€15M or 3%** (Art. 99(4)).
- **Misleading information to authorities** — up to **€7.5M or 1%** (Art. 99(5)).
- **SMEs and start-ups** — under Art. 99(6), the *lower* of the two figures applies.

**Who enforces:**

- The [**EU AI Office**](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/ai-office) — sole enforcer for general-purpose AI model providers (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Mistral, Meta).
- **National competent authorities** in each Member State — enforce against deployers. Some countries use their data-protection authority; some are naming new bodies. Designation is still in progress as of June 2026.
- For regulated finance, **ESMA, EBA, EIOPA** (and national equivalents like BaFin, AMF, the FCA's UK counterpart) are expected to lead supervision, coordinating with the AI Office.

*One-line takeaway:* enforcement against an investment firm will almost certainly come through your existing financial regulator, not a separate AI regulator.
