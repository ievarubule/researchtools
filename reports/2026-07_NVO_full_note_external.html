Novo Nordisk (NVO) — Research Note : is the obesity leader a bargain, or is Lilly simply winning?

The question

Novo leads the branded obesity market but just lost the head-to-head trial to Eli Lilly’s tirzepatide, and the stock has fallen to what looks like an ex-growth multiple. Is the leading franchise with a wounded next-gen pipeline a bargain — or is Lilly simply winning?

Company snapshot (at a glance)

A one-look profile of the company. All figures from Novo’s public filings (AR 2025 / Form 20-F / 2026 interims); prices are market quotes; market cap, EV and multiples are my own calculation, not quoted. As of 2026-07-06.

What it is: a Danish pharmaceutical company — one of the two global leaders in diabetes and obesity treatment. Almost all revenue comes from one molecule family, GLP-1 / semaglutide — sold as Ozempic and Rybelsus (diabetes) and Wegovy (obesity) — plus a large legacy insulin business and a small rare-disease arm. It is unusually vertically integrated (makes its own active ingredient and does its own fill-finish) and is controlled by a charitable foundation (Novo Holdings, ~77% of votes on ~28% of the economics), so ordinary shareholders cannot force a sale. Listed as NOVO-B (Copenhagen) and NVO (NYSE ADR, 1:1); reports in DKK, which is pegged to the euro.

The business

Novo is a Danish, R&D-driven pharma leader in diabetes and obesity, built on one molecular platform — GLP-1 / semaglutide (Ozempic, Rybelsus, Wegovy) — plus legacy insulin and a small rare-disease arm. By FY2025 revenue it is ~67% diabetes, ~27% obesity, ~6% rare disease — i.e. still mostly a diabetes company, with obesity the growth engine. It is foundation-controlled: the Novo Nordisk Foundation (via Novo Holdings) holds ~77% of the votes on ~28% of the economics — a patient, through-cycle owner (the Danish foundation-compounder model that can fund a heavy capex build-out public markets would punish), though one that limits minority-holder leverage and takeover optionality. All figures from Novo’s AR 2025, Form 20-F and 2026 interim filings.

The industry & competition

A secular, fast-growing market in a duopoly. The global branded GLP-1 obesity market grew ~104% in 2025 (Novo AR); Novo holds a 59.6% branded volume share, Lilly most of the rest. But on the momentum metrics Lilly is ahead: FY2025 revenue ~$65bn (+45%) vs Novo ~$47bn (+6–10%), Zepbound +175% vs Wegovy +31%, and tirzepatide beat semaglutide in the only head-to-head (REDEFINE 4: 23.0% vs 25.5%). The next battleground is oral: Novo’s Wegovy pill (a peptide — higher efficacy, harder to scale) vs Lilly’s orforglipron (a small molecule — ~12.4% weight loss, but far cheaper and easier to scale). US pricing is a real headwind — the semaglutide franchise was selected for IRA Medicare price negotiation (a ~71% cut on the Medicare channel from Jan 2027).

The financials & what’s priced in

An elite but turning signature: gross margin 84.7%→81.0%, operating margin ~44%→41.3%, and ROIC ~88.5% (2023) → 39.3% (2025) as a huge capacity build-out (capex DKK 6.3bn→60.1bn) plus the Catalent acquisition consumed cash — free cash flow was actually negative in 2024 before recovering. Growth fell from +25–31% to +6% (DKK) / +10% (CER).

What the price implies (reverse-DCF, my calculation, EUR basis at DKK 324): on normalised earnings, today’s price embeds only ~0–3% annual growth for a decade at an 8–10% cost of equity — a pessimistic path for a company that compounded ~21%/yr over 2021–25. But that only holds if capex normalises so free cash flow recovers; anchor on today’s depressed cash flow and the same price implies a big recovery is already required. So “cheap” is conditional, not automatic.

What the price is assuming (valuation & probability)

The market is making a very specific bet on Novo, and it is worth saying out loud.

Work backwards from the share price — a reverse-DCF — and at DKK 324 the market is paying for roughly 1% profit growth a year, for a decade. For a company that grew north of 20% a year through the GLP-1 boom, that is an ex-growth price.

What the price is paying for: Novo’s modelled equity value if earnings grow at 0%, 5% or 10% for a decade — the dashed line is today’s market cap, sitting just above the zero-growth bar.

Is 1% a reasonable expectation? To find out, I compared Novo against eleven of its actual peers — Lilly, Roche, Novartis, AstraZeneca, Sanofi, GSK, Merck, Pfizer, AbbVie, Amgen, Bristol Myers. Two things stood out. First, sustained fast growth is rare even in pharma: only a quarter of the group kept revenue growing above 10%, and the median grew about 4% a year. Second, and this is the striking part, Novo’s own recent record — about 20% a year — was the second fastest of the twelve. Yet the ~1% now baked into its price would rank tenth of twelve, below AbbVie and Bristol Myers, ahead of only Roche and Pfizer.

The peer evidence: revenue growth across the twelve-company pharma set. Novo’s own record (orange) sits second from the top; the ~1% its price implies (grey dashed) sits near the bottom.

So in the space of roughly a year, the market has repriced the industry’s number-two grower as a near-bottom decliner.

That does not make Novo cheap. It makes the whole question a single one: is that repricing right? The bear case is real and specific — Lilly is winning the head-to-head, and a US Medicare price cut lands in 2027. If those bite hard, a decliner’s price is fair. If they do not, the market is punishing a proven grower for one difficult stretch.

The difficult stretch, visible: 2025 was the first year gross, operating and net margins turned down together — still world-class levels, but the direction is the story.

Running the valuation five thousand times — growth drawn around the pharma-peer median, a Lilly-plus-Medicare bear included — the answer splits on a single assumption. If Novo’s currently depressed free cash flow normalises back toward its profit once the build-out ends, as management guides, fair value clears today’s price in about half of plausible futures: a coin flip, not a bargain. If cash flow stays where it is, it clears in none of them. The share price is not really the question. The capex hinge is.

The whole argument in one picture: 5,000 simulated futures. If cash flow normalises (blue), today’s DKK 324 cuts through the middle — a coin flip. If it stays as-is (grey), the price sits above every future. The gap between the two humps is the capex assumption.

I am not going to tell you which way it breaks, and I am certainly not going to tell you what to do about it. What I can do is show you exactly what the market is assuming, how that compares to Novo’s real peers, and where the argument actually turns — so you can weigh it yourself.

The bull case

Expectations are on the floor. A structurally elite franchise (81% gross margin, ~50% dividend payout) leads a market growing ~100%, is not a one-molecule company (real pipeline breadth in MASH, cardiovascular, rare disease), just posted the “strongest-ever GLP-1 volume launch” with the oral pill, and has a capex super-cycle that should roll off (per guidance) so cash flow recovers. If the franchise merely holds, the priced-in stagnation looks too harsh.

The bear case

A structurally impaired #2 losing a molecular arms race. Lilly is bigger, ~5× faster-growing, wins the head-to-head, and its oral small molecule could commoditise the tier Novo’s API-heavy pill depends on. The ROIC collapse may be the market pricing that incremental capital now earns less; a ~71% Medicare cut lands in 2027; and if the decline is structural, “cheap” is a value trap — on current cash flow the price already needs a recovery that may not come.

Through different investing lenses (perspectives, not a scorecard)

Different well-known investing frameworks — used here as lenses, not as endorsements — pull in different directions on this name, which is itself the point: - Quality lens: the financial signature is elite by pharma standards, but in 2025 it turned down across the board (margin, ROIC, cash conversion), and the moat — while real — is narrowing after the head-to-head loss. - Cycle / sentiment lens: the name has swung from euphoria to despair; the price now reflects pessimism, not optimism — a contrarian setup if the analysis holds. - Valuation lens: undemanding on multiples and on what the price implies (~0–3% growth priced in), but conditional on the cash-flow recovery. - Inversion / risk lens: the sharpest risk is that Novo becomes “the leader that loses the head-to-head and doesn’t recover” (the base-rate concern below), with US pricing and pipeline as the mechanisms; the mitigants are its incumbent share, breadth beyond one molecule, and the dividend.

(These are analytical perspectives to weigh — not ratings, not a recommendation.)

Base rates (context)

  • When a pharma #2/leader loses the pivotal head-to-head, does it recover the lead? Illustrative case (one iconic analogue, not a full dataset): in PD-1, BMS’s Opdivo lost the first-line lung head-to-head to Merck’s Keytruda and never recovered leadership — the winner compounded its lead. Directionally: the loser rarely re-takes the lead.
  • Do very-high-ROIC companies keep those returns? Cross-sectional history (Damodaran; Morgan Stanley): high ROIC fades toward the cost of capital, but top-quintile names often stabilise above it for years if the moat holds — full return to peak is unlikely, staying above cost of capital is common.
  • Are negotiated price cuts a cliff or a gradual drag? Historically a persistent drag, phased-in and partly offset by volume, rather than an overnight cliff to total revenue.

The questions that decide it

  1. Is the moat the molecule (losing) or the franchise (brand, oral tech, manufacturing, payers)?
  2. Is the ROIC / cash-flow collapse cyclical (capacity build-out) or structural (permanent competitive + capital intensity)?
  3. Can the oral pill + pipeline breadth + international volume offset Lilly and the 2027 price cut — i.e. does growth re-accelerate?
  4. How deep and permanent is the US net-price reset? The swing question is #3 — whether growth actually recovers. It is genuinely uncertain, and it is where the whole case is won or lost.

What I’m watching / what would change the view

  • US Wegovy weekly prescription share vs Zepbound — the earliest tell on the franchise.
  • 2026 landing vs guidance, and whether 2027 sales growth inflects positive.
  • Whether capex declines as guided and free cash flow recovers toward net income.
  • Whether the Medicare price cut spreads to commercial channels.
